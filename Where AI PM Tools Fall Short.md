# Where AI PM Tools Fall Short

The skeptical, evidence-based side of the picture — answering "if it's *not* improving workflows, why not?" Pairs with [[User Testimonials]]. For framing-level critique (vs. empirical), see [[Critiques of the Framing]].

## Hallucination & misattribution in synthesis

`[Synthesis]` Harvard's User Research Center tested [[Dovetail]]'s AI summary feature and found it **hallucinated** — e.g., falsely claiming the first participant had used EBSCO databases and searched JSTOR. Their verdict: too many errors for regular use; they would "stick to creating human-generated summaries instead." (Original page 403'd; EBSCO/JSTOR specifics corroborated across two independent search retrievals.)

## Real users on bolted-on AI underdelivering

> "The AI features are annoying, unreliable, inaccurate."
> — Olivia H., Principal Researcher (Capterra, [[Dovetail]])

> "They're all talk and no trousers. The AI summary does a poor job on transcripts and stops if the transcript is too long."
> — Jeremy P., Lead UX Researcher (Capterra, Dovetail)

> "I don't like the new AI features, it really doesn't make my job easier."
> — Valeria G., Sr. Product Designer (Capterra, Dovetail)

`[Synthesis]` On [[Productboard]] (G2 pros/cons), reviewers report the AI features "do not yet provide real added value" and that "many suggested links of insight notes to features are incorrect."

## Shallow input → shallow output

`[Synthesis]` Perspective AI's comparison argues analytics tools like Dovetail and Productboard AI "are brilliant at synthesizing what you've already collected, but inherit whatever shallow signal your collection layer captured" — e.g., the median in-product "tell us more" answer is only 4–7 words. "Your insight ceiling is set by your collection depth, not your analysis sophistication." (Vendor-adjacent — flagged in [[Evidence and Sourcing Notes]].)

## AI targets the wrong problem

> "'AI' won't automagically fix any of those problems." … "This 'AI triple threat' is not solving any problems. It is a solution looking for a problem."
> — Saeed Khan, product consultant, "'AI' is NOT going to fix Product Management"

`[Synthesis]` Khan's argument: AI automates *low-value artifact work* (mockups, docs) rather than the real dysfunctions — weak job definitions, under-skilled PMs, unclear objectives, insufficient research. "A bad system will defeat a good person every time."

## Hype deflation from a practicing PM

> "The realized efficiency gain is real … but it is not the 10x leadership panic emails keep assuming."
> — Abrar Abutouq, PM at Userpilot, "Is AI Product Management a Lie?" (estimates ~15–25% gains on execution work)

## "AI Slop" as a workflow failure mode

`[Synthesis]` A cross-source theme: PMs generate PRDs with AI, leads summarize with AI, engineers build from them, and nobody thinks through the problem at any step. As Abutouq puts it, PMs "forgot why they write PRDs in the first place. It was never about the document. It was about forcing yourself to think through the problem."

## What consistently disappoints (themes)

`[Inference]`
- Hallucination & misattribution in summaries.
- Generic, undifferentiated output ("an average of everything out there").
- Bolted-on AI in incumbent tools underwhelming vs. standalone LLMs.
- Output quality capped by input/context quality.
- "AI slop": volume without thought.
